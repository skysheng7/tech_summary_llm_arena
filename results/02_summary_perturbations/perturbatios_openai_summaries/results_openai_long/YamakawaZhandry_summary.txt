Yamakawa and Zhandry have demonstrated a significant finding concerning the capabilities of quantum computers compared to classical ones, specifically in the realm of NP *search* problems. Their work establishes that there exist certain NP *search* problems that quantum algorithms, operating within polynomial time, are capable of solving. This is a stark contrast to any classical probabilistic polynomial-time algorithm. Even if we were to allow classical probabilistic algorithms to have computationally unbounded power, but restrict them to making only polynomially many *classical* queries to an oracle, they would still be unable to solve these particular problems that quantum algorithms can efficiently handle. In essence, their research highlights a fundamental advantage quantum computation holds for a specific class of search-oriented computational tasks.

Furthermore, the researchers have devised a method for creating a *publicly verifiable* proof of quantumness. This proof system is remarkably efficient in terms of interaction. For adversaries who are considered uniformly random, the proof is essentially non-interactive, meaning there's no back-and-forth communication required. For adversaries who are non-uniform, meaning they have access to oracle-dependent advice, the proof requires only a two-message exchange that uses public coins. This means the proof of quantumness is readily accessible and verifiable by anyone, with minimal communication overhead depending on the adversary's nature.

Leveraging the very same underlying concepts and techniques, Yamakawa and Zhandry have also constructed cryptographic separations. These separations reveal scenarios where certain fundamental cryptographic primitives, such as one-way functions and even functions that are resistant to collisions (collision-resistant hash functions), remain secure against attackers who are limited in their querying capabilities using classical methods. However, these same primitives become easily breakable, specifically for attackers who employ quantum computation. The research goes even further, providing additional examples of such separations for digital signatures and for public-key encryption schemes that are secure against chosen-plaintext attacks (CPA-secure). The latter case is demonstrated under the assumption that a scheme which is secure against classical CPA attackers actually exists.

The core of these constructions, which enable these separations, hinges on a clever combination of outputs drawn from a random oracle with properties that are characteristic of strong error-correcting codes, particularly list recoverability. This combination makes it computationally infeasible for classical attackers to reverse or invert the operations. Meanwhile, a sophisticated procedure that utilizes the quantum Fourier transform is able to efficiently sample the preimages, effectively bypassing the classical infeasibility.
