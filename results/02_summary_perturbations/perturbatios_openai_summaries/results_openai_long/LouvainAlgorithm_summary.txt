The researchers Blondel, Guillaume, Lambiotte, and Lefebvre have put forth a proposal for a method that is designed to be a rapid heuristic approach, particularly well-suited for the task of identifying communities within exceedingly large networks. This proposed technique centers on the optimization of a metric known as modularity. The core of their algorithm involves a process that iteratively cycles through two primary phases. In the first phase, the algorithm engages in the local relocation of individual nodes. Specifically, it considers moving each node to an adjacent community if and when such a move results in an enhancement of the overall modularity score. This process of localized node movement is then followed by a second phase. In this second phase, the algorithm proceeds to aggregate the communities that have been formed or modified in the preceding step. These now-formed communities are essentially condensed or compressed into a smaller, more abstract representation of the network, which can be thought of as a "meta-network." This entire sequence of two phases – the local node movement and the subsequent community aggregation into a meta-network – is then repeated. This iterative repetition allows for the progressive construction of a hierarchical structure, effectively building layers of communities within the network.

The reason this particular methodology is capable of executing with such exceptional speed in practical applications stems from a couple of key efficiencies. Firstly, the computations required to determine the potential increases in modularity that result from moving individual nodes can be performed very efficiently. This means that the algorithm doesn't get bogged down in complex calculations during the local node movement phase. Secondly, and crucially, the network itself experiences a significant and rapid reduction in size after each aggregation step. As communities are combined into the meta-network, the overall scale of the problem being processed diminishes considerably. This dual benefit of efficient modularity gain calculations and rapid network shrinkage enables the method to operate with remarkable speed, to the point where it can effectively handle networks that are massive in scale, containing tens of millions or even hundreds of millions of nodes – a scale at which many previously existing methods have proven to be inadequate or have simply failed to perform.
